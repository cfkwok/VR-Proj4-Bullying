The Hallway

A crowd in the the school hallway has formed. The user is presented with a situation where a fellow classmate is being bullied and no one watching is doing anything about it. More times than not, people tend to not act on instances of bullying.

The Chase

Having now stood up to the bully, the user becomes the victim. This scene allows the user to see how the choices as a victim will result. The consequences are going to directly affect the user's situation now that the user is the victim.

The Classroom

By choosing to not interfere with the bullying situation, the user now can see the effects bullying has on the victim from an outside point of view. The user is given a second chance to aid the victim in the only way possible now.

BullyingSeen Through VR

Bullying affects hundreds of thousands students' lives throughout their personal and academic careers. There are various forms of bullying but we decided to protray some physical and verbal examples. People choose to bully others for different reasons but in our experience, the victim is bullied for his sexual orientation and appearance. We chose to include this in our experience because of the statistics we found regarding the high percentages of the students that were bullied for their sexual orientation. When presented with a bullying situation, your actions and decisions as a bystander or the victim can have a direct impact on the outcome. Making the right choices can lead to the correct consequences for the bully and more positive results for the victim.

Bullying StatisticsMeaningful Data

— Students who experience bullying are at increased risk for poor school adjustment, sleep difficulties, anxiety, and depression. (Center for Disease Control, 2015)

— One out of every four students (22%) report being bullied during the school year. (National Center for Educational Statistics, 2015)

— More than half of bullying situations (57 percent) stop when a peer intervenes on behalf of the student being bullied. (Hawkins, Pepler, and Craig, 2001)

— 81.9% of students who identify as LGBTQ were bullied in the last year based on their sexual orientation. (National School Climate Survey, 2011).

— 31.8% of LGBTQ students missed at least one entire day of school in the past month because they felt unsafe or uncomfortable. (National School Climate Survey, 2011).

— Students who experience bullying report that allying and supportive actions from their peers (such as spending time with the student, talking to him/her, helping him/her get away, or giving advice) were the most helpful actions from bystanders. (Davis and Nixon, 2010)

— 55% of students reported being bullied because of their looks (Davis and Nixon, 2010)
